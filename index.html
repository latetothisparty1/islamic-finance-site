Key Principles of Islamic Finance

Islamic finance is guided by **Sharia** (Islamic law). It operates on a bedrock of ethical and moral standards that promote fairness and social justice.

Common Products & Concepts

Murabaha (Cost-Plus Financing)

A contract where the bank purchases a specific asset (e.g., a car or house) and then sells it to the client at a predetermined profit margin, payable in installments.

Mudarabah (Profit-Sharing Partnership)

An investment partnership where one partner (the financier) provides all the capital, and the other (the entrepreneur) provides the labor and management. Profits are shared, but the financier bears the capital loss.

Sukuk (Islamic Trust Certificates)

These are Sharia-compliant financial instruments often referred to as 'Islamic Bonds.' They represent partial ownership in a tangible asset or project, rather than a debt obligation.
